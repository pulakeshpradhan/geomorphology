    Fluvial, Aeolian, and Glacial Landscapes: The Agents of Change

    1. Introduction: Agents of Erosion and Deposition

    While endogenetic forces create high-relief landforms, agents of gradation (water, wind, ice) work continuously to reshape them. These agents perform three functions: Erosion, Transportation, and Deposition. Each agent creates a unique set of landforms depending on the climate and rock structure.

    2. Fluvial Landscapes (Action of Running Water)

    Running water is the most significant geomorphic agent globally.

    A. Erosional Landforms

    • V-Shaped Valleys: Formed by downward cutting in the youthful stage of a river.
    • Waterfalls and Rapids: Formed where a river flows over rocks of varying resistance.
    • Potholes: Circular depressions in the riverbed carved by swirling pebbles.

    B. Depositional Landforms

    • Alluvial Fans: Fan-shaped deposits where a mountain stream enters a plain.
    • Floodplains: Flat areas adjacent to a river that are periodically submerged.
    • Deltas: Triangular deposits at the mouth of a river (e.g., Sundarbans Delta).

    3. Aeolian Landscapes (Action of Wind)

    Wind is a dominant agent in arid and semi-arid regions where vegetation is sparse.

    A. Erosional Landforms

    • Mushroom Rocks: Rocks with a narrow base and wide top, carved by sand-laden winds near the ground.
    • Deflation Hollows: Depressions in the desert floor created by the removal of loose sand.
    • Yardangs: Long, narrow ridges of rock parallel to the wind direction.

    B. Depositional Landforms

    • Sand Dunes: Mounds of sand (e.g., Barchans, Longitudinal dunes).
    • Loess: Fine, wind-blown silt deposited far from its source (e.g., Loess Plateau in China).

    4. Glacial Landscapes (Action of Moving Ice)

    Glaciers are "rivers of ice" that move under their own weight.

    A. Erosional Landforms

    • U-Shaped Valleys (Glacial Troughs): Formed when a glacier widens and deepens a V-shaped valley.
    • Cirques: Bowl-shaped depressions at the head of a glacier.
    • Aretes: Sharp, knife-like ridges between two cirques.

    B. Depositional Landforms

    • Moraines: Accumulations of dirt and rocks (till) deposited by a glacier.
    • Eskers: Long, winding ridges of sand and gravel deposited by sub-glacial streams.
    • Drumlins: Egg-shaped hills of glacial till.

    5. Coastal Landscapes (Action of Waves)

    The interaction between land and sea creates dynamic coastal features.

    A. Erosional Landforms

    • Cliffs and Wave-cut Platforms: Vertical rock faces and the flat benches at their base.
    • Sea Caves, Arches, and Stacks: Successive stages of erosional breakdown of a headland.

    B. Depositional Landforms

    • Beaches: Accumulations of sand/shingle along the shore.
    • Spits and Bars: Narrow ridges of sand extending into the sea or connecting two headlands.

    Descriptive Questions

    1. Explain the formation of a River Delta.

      Click to see Key Points

      • Occurs where a river meets a standing body of water (sea/lake).
      • The river's velocity drops to zero.
      • The load (sediment) is deposited.
      • Distributaries form as the river weaves through its own deposits.

    2. Differentiate between a V-shaped and a U-shaped valley.

      Click to see Key Points

      • V-shaped: Formed by vertical river erosion; steep sides and narrow bottom.
      • U-shaped: Formed by glacial scour; wide flat bottom and nearly vertical sides.
